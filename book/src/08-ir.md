# The IR

**What you'll understand:** why Nanuk's intermediate representation was
*extracted* from a working compiler rather than designed up front, how the
interpreter's step counter is welded to the lowering's cost model so tightly that
a divergence is always a bug, why the IR deliberately has *no* canonical byte
encoding, and how a v0 protobuf stays safely mutable through a single
commit-message escape hatch. We'll close with the Movi story — the time the test
suite reached down and made the language grow a primitive the machine had always
had.

Between the eDSL a person writes and the assembly the machine runs sits the Nanuk
IR: a protobuf format, ONNX-style, that every satellite tool hangs off. It is the
*hub*. The interpreter, the validators, the symbolic executor, the two backends —
all of them speak IR. This chapter is about the design decisions that made the hub
trustworthy, most of which are decisions *not* to do the obvious thing.

## Extracted, not speculated

The first and most consequential decision was to not design the IR first. The
project design states it as doctrine: *"IRs designed before their consumers exist
tend to be wrong."* So the pipeline was built in stages — first the eDSL compiled
straight to assembly, monolithically, with no IR at all; only once that worked was
the eDSL's *internal representation* hardened into a public `.proto`. The IR is
the fossil of a working compiler, not a whiteboard artifact.

The ONNX analogy is deliberate and precise: a serializable, language-neutral
interchange format sitting at *parser-semantics altitude* — extract a field,
branch on EtherType, emit a header vector — above instruction selection but below
syntax. N frontends, M tools, one hub. And because it was extracted, its fidelity
could be *proven*: the extraction wasn't done until a serialized-then-deserialized
IR lowered to byte-identical assembly as the direct path, and until a *hand-built*
IR proto — one never produced by the eDSL — could lower and run on the emulator,
proving the IR stands alone as an interchange format rather than as the eDSL's
private diary.

## Two validators, two disciplines

The IR carries two program shapes — `ParserProgram` and `MatchActionProgram` — and
each gets its own validator (`pp_validate`, `map_validate`). Both enforce a shared
value-id discipline that's SSA-ish: ids are unique per program, never zero, and
never cross a state boundary. But where they *differ* is the interesting part, and
it's a deliberate philosophical split.

The parser validator refuses to check ISA encoding limits. Its docstring is
explicit: *"ISA encoding limits (16-bit immediates, 11-bit EXT offsets, 4 GPRs)
are deliberately not checked here — the IR sits above the ISA; those surface in
lower.py."* So a parser IR with an over-wide immediate is *valid IR*; it only fails
when you try to lower it. The MAP validator does the opposite: it enforces
encoding ranges at the IR level, because *"the MatchActionProgram IR is
deliberately byte-granular and offset-bounded like its engine"* — 10-bit signed
offsets, 16-bit immediates, 1-to-8-byte accesses, all checked at validate time.

This is a real, unresolved tension, and the honest thing is to name it rather than
paper over it. The two validators embody two answers to "what does valid IR mean?"
— the parser says "structurally well-formed, ranges are the backend's problem," the
MAP says "if the engine can't encode it, it isn't valid." The SIIT work (Chapter
13) caught the seam: the newly added parser `Movi` op is range-checked only at
lower, following the parser doctrine, so a malformed parser IR *"passes validate,
passes interp, passes symex, and fails only at lower — a three-way disagreement on
what valid IR means."* Harmless for well-formed programs, a trap for anyone
hand-writing parser IR. We ship it, documented, as the price of two coherent
doctrines rather than one muddled compromise.

## The step counter is welded to the cost model

Here is the doctrine that paid off more than any other in the IR layer. Nanuk has
an IR *interpreter* — an independent implementation of the IR's semantics, used to
differentially test the compiler (`interp(IR)` against `emulate(lower(IR))`). An
interpreter and a compiled program agreeing on the *result* is a good test. Nanuk
demands more: they must agree on the *step count*, instruction for instruction.

The mechanism is that the interpreter doesn't *count* instructions after the fact —
it *re-derives* the count by ticking its step counter in exact lockstep with what
the lowering would emit. A single-instruction op ticks once. The load-bearing case
is dispatch: each case lowers to a `MOVI`+`BEQ` pair, so the interpreter ticks
*twice per case* — with the comments `# MOVI` and `# BEQ` right there in the tick
calls. An op that lowers to nothing — a `mark` that re-anchors the cursor without
emitting a `SETHDR` — ticks zero times. The interpreter's step count *is* the
lowering's instruction count, computed independently and forced to match.

The docstrings make the contract mutual and explicit. The interpreter: *"Step
accounting follows the v0 lowering's cost model instruction-for-instruction... If
the lowering's cost model ever changes, this file must change with it."* The
lowering: *"the cost model this lowering implies is mirrored
instruction-for-instruction by the interpreter — change one, change both, and the
differential tests will catch you if you don't."* The tightest assertion of all
lives in the parity tests: `interp.steps == iss.steps == len(iss.trace)` — the
interpreter's step count equals the simulator's step count equals the number of
instructions actually executed. Three independent computations of the same number,
pinned equal.

Why go to this trouble? Because it turns the step counter into a *free
verification signal*. Two implementations that agree on a verdict might both be
wrong in the same place. Two implementations forced to agree on a step-by-step
execution *length* are far harder to fool: a lowering that emits an extra
instruction, or an interpreter that models a construct as cheaper than it lowers,
diverges immediately and points at the exact construct. The doctrine costs
discipline (every cost-model change touches two files) and repays it in bugs that
can't hide.

## No canonical byte encoding, on purpose

Most interchange formats have a canonical serialization — a blessed byte sequence
you can hash and compare. Nanuk's IR deliberately does not, and the reasoning is
recorded as intentional: *"The canonical artifact of record is assembly/binary; IR
equality is semantic. Cross-language golden tests must compare parsed messages or
lowered output, never encoded IR bytes — they'd be flaky by design and would
silently crown one encoder canonical."*

The point is subtle. proto3's wire format isn't deterministic across
implementations, so if a golden test compared *IR bytes*, it would be asserting
that one particular protobuf library's output is the truth — and that assertion
would break the day another language's protobuf serializer ordered fields
differently, even though the two IRs mean exactly the same thing. By declaring IR
equality to be *semantic* (compare parsed messages, or compare what they lower to),
Nanuk refuses to elevate an encoder to canonical status. The `.proto` even declines
`buf format` enforcement, for a kindred reason: auto-formatting *"would flatten the
hand-aligned teaching comments"* — the IR schema is written to be *read*, and
neither its bytes nor its whitespace are the contract. Its meaning is.

## The [ir-breaking] hatch: a mutable v0

An extracted IR is going to keep changing while its consumers stabilize, so a
frozen schema would be a lie in v0. But an unguarded schema invites silent breakage.
Nanuk splits the difference with `buf breaking` checks and a single, deliberate
escape hatch. The `buf.yaml` states the policy: *"v0 is the mutable dev-phase
contract: breaking it is allowed, but only consciously — put `[ir-breaking]` in the
commit message to pass CI. At the v1 freeze the escape hatch goes away and breaking
changes need a new vN package."*

CI enforces exactly that: it runs `buf breaking` against the previous commit
*unless* a commit message in the range contains the literal string `[ir-breaking]`,
in which case it prints "commit declares [ir-breaking]; skipping breaking check"
and moves on. A breaking change is never blocked, but it is never *silent* — it
leaves a permanent, greppable mark in the history saying "here, on purpose, the IR
contract moved." Real commits carry it: `feat(ir)!: metadata window ops ...
[ir-breaking]`. The hatch is scheduled for deletion at the v1 freeze, at which
point breaking the IR will require a whole new versioned package rather than an
edit. It's the minimum ceremony that keeps a mutable format honest.

## The Movi story: the suite made the language grow

The chapter's best illustration of the extracted-IR philosophy is a primitive that
appeared because a test demanded it. Writing the SIIT eDSL twins (Chapter 13) hit a
wall the hand-written assembly didn't: the parser needed to write a header-present
bitmap as a *literal* into a metadata slot, and the parser eDSL had **no way to
materialize a constant**. Its only value sources were `extract` (packet bits) and
`load_md` — every shipping parser twin had only ever stored *extracted* values. The
eight bitmap literals mix L3 and L4 bits and can't be built from any known field by
shifting.

The tell was that the machine already had the capability. The ISA has `MOVI`, and
the parser lowering emits it *constantly* — but only as an internal detail, to
stage dispatch and compare constants into a reserved scratch register. The
capability existed one layer down and had simply never been surfaced as a value the
IR could *name*. As the lab notes put it: *"this wasn't 'the machine can't'; it was
'the language never asked.'"*

The fix was a full mini-vertical, mirroring the MAP side's existing constant
primitive exactly: a `Movi` op added to the parser IR proto, the pb2 regenerated,
threaded through validate, lower, interp, and symex, and surfaced in the eDSL as
`s.const(imm)` — the *same spelling* the MAP eDSL already used, for vocabulary
parity. Then the twins closed cleanly. The proto comment tells the whole story in
two sentences: *"The parser twin of MapConst: the machine has always had MOVI, but
the parser IR only used it internally to stage compare constants — this surfaces it
as a first-class value, so a parser can emit a literal to the md window."*

This is the same shape as the benchmark suite's reg-reg ALU (Chapter 12), and it's
the same doctrine: *the suite drives the language.* A benchmark that only ever
passes isn't exercising the language's edges. The SIIT twins passed *behaviorally*
long before they could be *written*, and closing that gap made the IR — the hub —
one primitive richer. The IR grew the way it was always meant to: not from a
whiteboard, but from a real program that couldn't be expressed until it did.

## Where this bit us

The IR grew *more general* than its design sketch in ways worth flagging, because
they show extraction working as intended. The design doc imagined an in-place
`CsumUpdate { hdr_id, byte_offset }`; the shipped `Csum` is a generic RFC-1071
range checksum returning a value over a runtime length — the primitive got smaller
and more composable than the accelerator the doc drew. The doc's `MapSend` carried
a port-bitmap value id; the shipped `MapSend` reserves that field with the comment
*"was bitmap_value_id — egress meaning moved to the metadata window,"* because
routing the egress decision through metadata turned out cleaner than baking it into
the terminator. And the design-doc names themselves drifted — `MapProgram` became
`MatchActionProgram` in an `[ir-breaking]` rename — so the design docs and the
shipped proto disagree on identifiers, and the *code* names are the ones to trust.

Every one of these is the extracted-IR doctrine paying out: the format we shipped
is the one the consumers actually needed, discovered by building them, not the one
we guessed before they existed. The hatch that let the schema keep moving, the step
counter that caught cost-model drift, the refusal to canonicalize bytes — they're
all in service of the same bet, that an IR is best *found* in a working compiler
rather than *designed* into an empty one.
